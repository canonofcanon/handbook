01 — INTRODUCTION

Introduction

SurtzMedia is a creative production studio built around strong work, thoughtful collaboration, and respect for the people who make the work possible.

This handbook is intended to make expectations clear so our team can work with flexibility, accountability, and trust. It is not meant to answer every possible situation. When something is unclear, employees should use good judgment, communicate early, and ask a supervisor, the Executive Director, or company leadership for direction.

We take pride in being a flexible workplace that promotes a healthy work-life balance. We also go to great lengths to align workloads with schedules whenever possible.

At the same time, in order to ensure we serve our clients and maintain our standards of excellence, our work is structured around workload and outcomes first, and schedule second. Client deadlines, production needs, shoot schedules, travel, and company obligations may sometimes require schedule adjustments, overtime, or work outside a typical day.

A Note on Balance

This does not mean we value anyone’s health or personal life any less. Setting clear expectations is part of protecting the health of the entire team. If you have concerns about workload, repeated long hours, burnout, or schedule strain, speak with your supervisor as early as possible.

02 — HOW TO USE THIS HANDBOOK

How to Use This Handbook

This handbook is a shared reference for how we work together at SurtzMedia. It covers expectations around schedule, communication, time off, shoots, comp days, office use, post-production practices, confidentiality, gear, benefits, sales opportunities, and internal company policies.

The goal is not to create unnecessary rules. The goal is to make expectations visible, reduce confusion, protect our team, protect our clients, and make it easier to do good work.

If a policy is unclear, ask before acting. Some things only require notification. Other things require actual approval. When approval is required, telling someone is not enough — a request is not approved until it has been confirmed by the appropriate supervisor, the Executive Director, or company leadership.

Approval is required before:

  • Changing scheduled work hours
  • Taking PTO or planned time off
  • Using a comp day
  • Borrowing company gear
  • Sharing company work publicly
  • Sending draft links outside the internal team and immediate client team
  • Giving anyone access to the office, keys, door codes, company accounts, or company files
  • Using company equipment, time, client materials, or company relationships for outside work
  • Contacting a sales lead on behalf of SurtzMedia
  • Making purchases, rentals, or reimbursable expenses outside an approved process
  • Using Vimeo’s Replace Video feature for client review cuts
  • Uploading client or company materials to unapproved AI, transcription, enhancement, conversion, or file-sharing tools

This handbook is subject to applicable federal, state, and local law. If any policy conflicts with applicable law, the law controls.

03 — AVAILABILITY, SCHEDULES, BREAKS, AND PTO

Availability, Schedules, Breaks, and PTO

The standard expectation is that full-time employees work a minimum of 40 hours per week and are available during normal business hours, generally 9:00 a.m. to 5:00 p.m.

Salaried employees may work slightly different hours based on role, workload, production schedules, or project needs. However, in-office availability during normal 9–5 business hours is still expected unless otherwise approved.

Hourly employees are expected to be in the office from 9:00 a.m. to 5:00 p.m. unless they are scheduled for a shoot, approved for a schedule adjustment, using approved time off, or otherwise directed by a supervisor.

Schedule Adjustments

Reasonable shifts in start or end time may occasionally be needed — for example, arriving early in order to leave early for a personal commitment.

Schedule adjustments should be requested at least 48 hours in advance through go.surtzmedia.com/pto. Exceptions may be made when circumstances genuinely require flexibility, based on the situation, not on forgetting to communicate.

Lunch and Breaks

Employees receive a 30-minute paid lunch and may take 10-minute breaks as needed under the company’s flexible break policy.

Additional or extended breaks are totally fine — we all should take a lap around the river or stop by the creamery. We want employees to take the time they need to foster creativity, community, and sustainable work. Extended or additional break time should be made up the same day unless arranged in advance with a supervisor.

05 — COMP DAYS

Comp Days

SurtzMedia’s production schedule sometimes requires employees to work on days that would normally be personal time, including weekends, holidays, and other non-standard production days. Comp days are intended to recognize that sacrifice by giving employees replacement time off.

A comp day is not PTO, vacation, sick time, or a bonus. It is a scheduling tool used to restore rest time when an employee works a company-required shoot, production event, or other approved work commitment outside the employee’s normal schedule.

Separate From PTO

Comp days are separate from PTO. Using a comp day does not reduce the employee’s PTO balance.

Earning Comp Days

Comp days must be approved by a manager or company leadership. Employees should not assume that after-hours, weekend, or self-directed work automatically earns a comp day. Comp days are generally intended for company-directed work, not voluntary personal projects, outside creative work, networking, or work for another filmmaker or company.

05 — COMP DAYS

Using and Tracking Comp Days

Using Comp Days

Comp days should be requested through go.surtzmedia.com/pto. Employees may use comp days in half-day increments, and may not use increments smaller than a half day unless specifically approved.

Comp days are intended for rest, recovery, personal time, or life obligations. A comp day may not be used to perform SurtzMedia work, client work, freelance work, outside professional work, outside production work, or other work activities unless specifically approved.

Employees may combine multiple accrued comp days for a longer absence, including travel or vacation-style time off, with advance approval. Requests to use three or more consecutive comp days should be submitted as early as possible, preferably at least two weeks in advance.

Limits, Expiration, and Tracking

Unless otherwise approved by company leadership:

  • Employees may bank up to 6 comp days at one time.
  • Comp days expire 60 days after they are earned.
  • Employees approaching the cap should work with their manager to schedule time off.
  • Exceptions may be approved during unusually busy production periods.

Employees are responsible for logging and requesting comp days accurately through go.surtzmedia.com/pto. The request should include the date earned, project or shoot name, reason earned, amount earned, and date requested for use.

Comp days are separate from legally required wages, overtime, or other compensation. Non-exempt employees will be paid in accordance with applicable wage and hour laws, including overtime rules where applicable. No employee may approve their own comp time.

06 — RESPECTFUL WORKPLACE AND RAISING CONCERNS

Respectful Workplace and Raising Concerns

SurtzMedia expects employees, contractors, freelancers, guests, and company representatives to treat one another with respect.

Harassment, discrimination, bullying, retaliation, intimidation, or abusive conduct are not acceptable. This applies in the office, on set, while traveling, at client locations, in company communication channels, online, and anywhere else work is being performed or represented.

Employees should raise concerns promptly when something feels unsafe, inappropriate, unfair, or unclear. Concerns may be brought to a supervisor, the Executive Director, or company leadership.

Good-faith concerns will not be punished. Retaliation for raising a concern, participating in a review, or reporting a problem is not acceptable.

Mistakes happen. When they do, the first goal is usually to clarify expectations, fix the issue, and improve the process. Repeated issues, avoidable communication failures, policy violations, missed responsibilities, or conduct concerns may result in coaching, loss of privileges, changes in responsibilities, or further action as appropriate.

Why It Matters

Flexibility and trust are part of the culture here. Accountability is what protects that culture.

07 — COMMUNICATION STANDARDS

Communication Standards

Employees are expected to monitor company communication channels during working hours and respond appropriately. This includes Slack, email, client platforms, project management tools, shared calendars, review platforms, phone calls, texts, and any other channel being used for company work.

Not every message requires an immediate full answer. In many cases, a quick acknowledgment is enough. What matters is that the team is not left guessing.

Messages involving client deliverables, shoot logistics, deadlines, approvals, missing assets, schedule changes, gear, travel, or anything time-sensitive should be treated as urgent until clarified.

Client Response Time

Client communications must receive a reply within 24 hours. This applies to emails, calls, texts, review comments, client platform messages, and other client-facing communication.

A complete answer is not always required within 24 hours. If a full answer is not ready, send a short acknowledgment and a realistic timeline.

Example Acknowledgments

“Thanks for sending this over. I’m confirming a few details internally and will get back to you tomorrow.”

“Received — we’re reviewing this now and will follow up with next steps.”

If the employee most relevant to a client communication is off, unavailable, traveling, on a shoot, using PTO, using a comp day, or otherwise unable to reply, that employee is responsible for coordinating with a supervisor or teammate so the client still receives a timely response. The client should not be left waiting simply because the primary person is unavailable.

08 — ETHICAL STORYTELLING

Ethical Storytelling

Our work tells true stories about real people — clients, beneficiaries, founders, clergy, athletes, families, employees, students, communities, and others who trust us with access to their lives.

The films and series we make may live for years across theaters, broadcasts, websites, social platforms, campaigns, events, and internal client use. That reach is a privilege, and it carries responsibilities that do not change based on the client, budget, platform, or genre.

What It Means

Ethical storytelling means we tell stories that are compelling, accurate, respectful, and worthy of the trust placed in us.

08 — ETHICAL STORYTELLING

Core Principles

Informed Consent

Subjects should understand what they are agreeing to, how they may be portrayed, where the work may be shown, and how long it may live. A signed release is not the same as informed consent. Whenever possible, explain use cases plainly and in the subject’s first language.

Dignity

People should be portrayed as full human beings, not as symbols, archetypes, props, or tools for an argument. Avoid framing that strips agency or reduces a person to a problem, trauma, need, conversion, success story, or emotional beat. The test is simple: would the subject recognize themselves in the portrayal and feel respected by it?

Truthfulness

Editing may compress reality. It should not distort it. Do not knowingly fabricate scenes, manipulate chronology, misrepresent cause and effect, or present composite characters as real. Reenactments, recreations, AI-generated elements, and other constructed material must be disclosed when they could affect how an audience understands the truth of the piece.

Awareness of Power

The storyteller often holds more power than the subject: access, final cut, distribution, money, technical expertise, and control over context. Acknowledge that imbalance. Do not exploit it, especially when working with children, grieving people, marginalized communities, people in crisis, or people who depend on the client organization for services, employment, education, housing, immigration support, or other needs.

Do No Harm

Consider the second-order consequences of the work. Could this footage endanger someone, damage a community, expose private information, or follow a subject for years? “They signed a release” is not a sufficient answer. When in doubt, protect the person first.

08 — ETHICAL STORYTELLING

Core Principles, continued

Voice and Authorship

Ask whose perspective is framing the story. Whenever possible, let subjects speak for themselves rather than being spoken about. Be honest internally about whose film, campaign, or message this really is.

Context

Do not strip images, quotes, emotions, or moments from the surrounding reality that gives them meaning. A single tear out of context is not a documentary moment. It is a manipulation.

Transparency With the Audience

Be honest about point of view, funding, access, recreations, AI-generated elements, and any other factors that materially affect how the audience understands what they are watching.

Reciprocity

Subjects should receive something real from participating. At minimum, they deserve respect, clarity, and a copy of the work when feasible. Ideally, they experience a genuine relationship that outlasts the shoot.

Responsibility After Release

The story does not end at release. When appropriate, stay reachable and be willing to answer for the work after it is out in the world.

08 — ETHICAL STORYTELLING

Working With High-Stakes Subjects

Some projects carry sharper risk and require extra care.

Vulnerable Subjects

This may include survivors of trauma, addiction, or abuse; undocumented people; minors; people in active grief or crisis; and people sharing highly personal or sensitive information. Err toward protection, anonymity, or transparent composite framing when needed. When in doubt, do not shoot it.

Subjects Who Cannot Easily Decline

Some people cannot meaningfully say no in the same way an unrelated subject can. This includes people who depend on a client organization for services, employment, education, housing, immigration support, healthcare, charity, pastoral care, or other forms of help. A useful test: would this person still say yes if the camera were not connected to the help, job, program, or community they are receiving?

Cross-Cultural and International Work

When filming across cultural lines, the question is not only “did we get consent?” It is also “whose story is this, and who is telling it?” When appropriate, hire local crew, seek local input, share editorial context, and credit properly.

Faith and Conscience

Much of our work involves people sharing their interior lives — conversion, prayer, suffering, vocation, grief, hope, confession, or conscience. Treat those disclosures with deep care. Do not use them merely to score a moment.

Children

Default to extra protection when working with children. Obtain written parental or guardian consent, limit identifying information where appropriate, and provide a reasonable path to review or remove footage when appropriate and feasible as the child grows up.

08 — ETHICAL STORYTELLING

Client-Driven Pressures

Every client has goals — fundraising, evangelization, recruitment, audience growth, theatrical release, brand awareness, or institutional reputation. Those goals are legitimate, and we are paid to serve them. They cross a line when they ask us to:

  • Inflate the client’s role in someone else’s story
  • Imply impact or causation we cannot substantiate
  • Frame beneficiaries, parishioners, students, clients, or participants as helpless before the client arrived
  • Trade a subject’s dignity for an emotional beat that moves donations, conversions, enrollment, or ticket sales
  • Remove context in a way that makes the story more useful but less true
The Right Move

The right move when these pressures appear is not automatically to refuse the work. It is to find the truthful version of the story that still serves the client’s goal. There almost always is one. Urgency, hope, conviction, and emotion can be built through truth. They do not require pity or distortion.

08 — ETHICAL STORYTELLING

Practical Production Standards

Pre-Production

Discuss editorial standards with the client before production whenever possible. Identify people likely to appear on camera, flag vulnerable populations, and agree on protections in writing when needed. Build a clear list of what we will not shoot, imply, include, or expose.

On Set

Re-explain consent at the moment of filming, not only on paper. Subjects can stop at any time without consequence. Pay particular attention to children and vulnerable subjects, who may defer to adults, authority figures, clients, clergy, teachers, coaches, employers, or crew members around them.

In the Edit

Review cuts against these standards. A second set of eyes, ideally someone outside the immediate production, should screen sensitive work for dignity, accuracy, context, and unintended harm before the client sees a locked picture.

Release

Show subjects the final piece before public release whenever feasible, especially for sensitive stories. Provide a copy of the work when appropriate. Document where the work will live and for how long when the usage is unusually sensitive or high-stakes.

After Release

Maintain a process for handling reasonable takedown, amendment, or concern requests from subjects. Not every request can or will be granted, but every concern should be taken seriously and reviewed with care.

08 — ETHICAL STORYTELLING

A Quick Test Before Locking a Cut

Before locking a sensitive story, ask:

  • Would the subject recognize themselves in this and feel respected?
  • Is the client’s role represented accurately, or inflated?
  • Could anything here endanger a person or community now or later?
  • Is anything in the cut true only because of how it is edited?
  • Are we using emotion to reveal truth, or to manipulate?
  • If a journalist scrutinized our access, consent, and claims, would we be comfortable with what they found?

For Further Reading

These principles are informed by common standards in documentary, nonprofit, humanitarian, and editorial storytelling, including the Documentary Producers Alliance ethical guidelines; POV / ITVS editorial standards; Honest Truths by Patricia Aufderheide (Center for Media & Social Impact, American University); Radi-Aid / SAIH guidelines for ethical communication; and Bond’s People in the Pictures ethical guidelines for content gathering.

Closing Note

These principles are not a checklist to clear. They are a posture to hold. When client goals, craft, and the dignity of the subject pull in different directions — and they will — the tiebreaker is the dignity of the person on the other side of the lens.

09 — CONFIDENTIALITY

Confidentiality

We want to celebrate our work and the people who make it. At the same time, all work should be considered confidential by default.

This includes company work, client information, internal conversations, project files, drafts, budgets, creative concepts, footage, edits, production details, screenshots, unreleased work, behind-the-scenes materials, and anything else related to active or unreleased projects.

Confidentiality applies inside and outside the office, including in person, online, on social media, in personal portfolios, and in conversations with friends, family, other filmmakers, vendors, freelancers, or clients.

Do not assume something is public just because it feels casual or because you worked on it. When in doubt, do not share. Ask first.

10 — CLOSED SETS

Closed Sets

All sets should be considered closed sets by default. Employees may not publicly share behind-the-scenes materials, drafts, screenshots, edits, client information, unreleased work, set photos, or other project materials unless they have express permission from company leadership or the appropriate project lead.

For behind-the-scenes content from a client set, public sharing requires approval from SurtzMedia, the client, and other crew members who may appear in or be affected by the content. Permission should be coordinated through the company.

11 — DRAFT LINKS AND OUTGOING LINKS

Draft Links and Outgoing Links

Video draft links may not be sent to anyone outside the immediate client team and internal SurtzMedia team without express permission.

Video drafts may be shown in person to family, friends, or similar personal contacts only if the content is not sensitive and doing so does not violate client expectations, confidentiality, or common sense.

When sending an outgoing link to a client, contractor, freelancer, or other outside party, test the link in a guest window before sending it.

Before sending any client-facing link, confirm:

  • The link opens correctly in a guest window.
  • The correct file, video, folder, or page is being shared.
  • Access permissions are correct.
  • Passwords or privacy settings are intentional.
  • Download settings are correct.
  • Titles and filenames are spelled correctly.
  • Nothing confidential, unrelated, or internal-only is visible.
  • The client is receiving the correct version.

This applies to review links, folders, files, forms, Vimeo links, Vimeo Showcases, Google Drive links, Frame.io links, and any other shared resource.

12 — COMPANY AI POLICY

Company AI Policy

SurtzMedia supports thoughtful use of technology, including artificial intelligence, when it serves human dignity, strengthens creative work, and helps people do better work with greater care.

In reference to the principles of Pope Leo XIV’s encyclical Magnifica Humanitas, also known in English as Magnificent Humanity, our approach is human-first: AI should serve people, not replace the dignity, judgment, creativity, or responsibility of the human person.

Our Principles

We support the use of AI insofar as it serves human dignity. We encourage, and may require, the use of approved AI-enabled tools for the purpose of further unlocking our own creative potential — especially tools that accelerate efficiency, reduce bottlenecks, support organization, improve accessibility, and foster better creative thinking.

We will not require any employee to use generative AI. For purposes of this policy, “generative AI” means tools that generate or substantially rewrite creative work, including text, images, audio, video, voice, design, or other original expressive material. The company may still require use of approved non-generative or assistive AI tools, such as tools for search, transcription, organization, quality control, automation, media management, or internal workflow efficiency.

Human Judgment Comes First

AI tools may assist the work. They do not own the work. Employees remain responsible for accuracy, judgment, tone, ethics, confidentiality, and final output. AI should not be used to impersonate a real person, fabricate quotes, invent facts, mislead a client, misrepresent a subject, or create work that violates our ethical storytelling standards.

Client and Company Materials

Do not upload client footage, transcripts, audio, scripts, creative documents, private documents, unreleased work, internal company materials, or confidential information to AI tools, transcription tools, audio enhancement tools, file conversion tools, or similar services unless the tool and use have been approved for that project. This applies whether the tool is free, paid, browser-based, desktop-based, or part of another software platform. When in doubt, ask first.

13 — COMPANY ACCOUNTS, PASSWORDS, AND FILES

Company Accounts, Passwords, and Files

Company accounts, passwords, access credentials, and login information must be handled carefully.

Employees may not share passwords, account access, or login credentials with unauthorized people. Company accounts should only be used for approved company purposes.

14 — OFFICE ACCESS, KEYS, AND GUESTS

Office Access, Keys, and Guests

The office is for all of us. It is also a privilege. Off-hours office access is permitted, including for non-work purposes, as long as it is used responsibly.

Employees should notify the Executive Director when intending to use the office outside normal work hours, and are responsible for leaving the space clean, secure, and ready for the next person. Off-hours use must still comply with all office, safety, guest, confidentiality, alcohol, access, and building policies.

Keys, Door Codes, and Access Credentials

Office keys, door codes, and access credentials may only be issued through the company’s approved process. Anyone issued a key must sign the company’s Key Issuance & Responsibility Agreement. Keys are for the assigned keyholder’s personal use only and may not be duplicated, loaned, transferred, or provided to any other person for any reason.

Lost, stolen, duplicated, or unaccounted-for keys must be reported to SurtzMedia immediately. A keyholder may be financially responsible for costs associated with rekeying or recoring entry door locks, locksmith labor, replacement keys, and any additional security measures deemed necessary by the company, where permitted by law.

Keys must be returned immediately upon termination, resignation, the end of an engagement with SurtzMedia, company request, or completion of the project or contract for which access was granted. SurtzMedia reserves the right to revoke key access at any time, for any reason, with or without cause.

Two Hard Rules

The office should never be left unlocked when unoccupied, for any period of time. Guests may not be left unattended in the suite, and may not have keys, door codes, or access credentials to let themselves in or out. No alcohol is permitted when any Hosted Party, guest, or client is using the premises.

15 — OFFICE STANDARDS AND BUILDING ETIQUETTE

Office Standards and Building Etiquette

Our team is permitted to play music and audio as part of our work. However, we must avoid vibrations or noise disturbances that may be offensive or disruptive to neighboring tenants. Use headphones for heavy bass editing or loud audio work.

Hosted guests are subject to stricter rules. Loud music is prohibited during hosted sessions. If outside guests are in the studio, keep volume levels controlled.

Parking and Loading

SurtzMedia has rights to one dedicated parking space on the southwest side of the building, reserved for the company vehicle. Personal vehicles should not park in the designated company spot except when arranged in advance for an extended shoot. Employees should use available common area parking or public parking.

People may use the loading area at the very front of the building for loading and unloading, but vehicles should not be left there for more than 15 minutes. Do not block sidewalks, building entries, or access routes when loading or unloading gear.

Trash and Facilities

We are responsible for taking out our own trash. Trash must be taken to the building’s designated disposal area just north of Bird Is The Word. Do not use the dumpster across the street by the gym. Dumpster lids and receptacles must be kept closed at all times.

We are responsible for cleaning our own suite. Clean up after yourself, especially food, drinks, dishes, gear, and workstation messes.

Building Etiquette and Workstations

Do not solicit business from other tenants in the building without prior written approval. Do not store equipment, boxes, personal items, or other materials in hallways, common areas, or outside the suite door. Treat shared building areas respectfully and leave them clean.

Employees may decorate their personal workspace as they see fit, as long as the content is tasteful and workplace appropriate. No permanent modifications are allowed without approval — do not paint, drill, mount, or use adhesive that could leave a mark unless approved in advance.

  • Never leave a space heater unattended while on.
  • Never leave a space heater plugged in while unattended, even if it is off.
  • Always unplug space heaters when not in use.

Anyone using a space heater accepts personal responsibility for the safety of that heater and its use.

Rules at a Glance

At the end of the buffer period, the folder may be deleted and a new monthly folder created. Do not rely on local folders or the temporary folder as permanent storage.

28 — LEAD COMMISSION POLICY

Lead Commission Policy

SurtzMedia wants to encourage employees to bring strong client relationships and meaningful projects into the business. Employees may be eligible for a revenue commission when they originate or bring in a qualifying lead that becomes a signed SurtzMedia client project.

Eligible employee leads may qualify for a 10% revenue commission. Commissions are based on actual dollars received by SurtzMedia, not the proposal amount, contract amount, invoice amount, or any other amount that has not yet been paid.

Program Window

This commission program currently expires September 10, 2026, unless extended, replaced, or modified by company leadership.

Examples may include a former school contact reaching out about an admissions video, an employee contacting a local business that could benefit from video storytelling, or an employee helping establish a new relationship that becomes a signed SurtzMedia project.

Final determination of whether a lead qualifies rests with the Executive Director. If two people are jointly responsible for the connection, the commission may be split between them, such as 5% and 5%, unless otherwise approved.

Approval, Payment, and Ownership

Approval Before Contact
